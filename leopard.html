Sri Lankan Leopard

The Sri Lankan leopard (Panthera pardus kotiya) is a magnificent big cat species native to the island of Sri Lanka. Renowned for its distinctive golden-yellow coat adorned with dark rosettes, this elusive carnivore inhabits a variety of habitats, from dense jungles to dry grasslands. Classified as endangered, the Sri Lankan leopard faces threats such as habitat loss and human-wildlife conflict, making conservation efforts crucial for its survival.

Udawalawe National Park

While Udawalawe is more famous for its elephant population, leopards are also present in the park. It's located in the southern part of the island.

Horton Plains National Park

his park is known for its scenic beauty and is home to a variety of wildlife, including leopards. It is situated in the central highlands.

Kumana National Park

Located in the southeast of Sri Lanka, Kumana is renowned for its birdlife, but leopards can also be found in the area.

Threats for Leopards

Leopards face various threats, primarily due to human activities and the resulting impact on their habitats and interactions with people.

Poaching and Illegal Wildlife Trade

Leopards in Sri Lanka have been subjected to Habitat loss and fragmentation occur due to human activities such as deforestation, agriculture expansion, and infrastructure development. As a result, the natural habitats of leopards in Sri Lanka.are shrinking and becoming fragmented



Consequences:

Population Decline.
Ecosystem Disruption.
Genetic Diversity Loss.
Social Instability.

Mechanisms to Reduce the Threat:

Strengthening Law Enforcement International

and Education Demand Reduction

Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

Clearing of forests for agriculture, logging, and urban development reduces the available habitats for leopards. As a result, their territories become smaller and more fragmented.

Consequences:

Reduced Living Space
solation and Genetic Bottleneck
Increased Human-Wildlife Conflict.

Mechanisms to Reduce the Threat:

Strengthening Law Enforcement International
Collaboration Community Involvement
and Education Demand Reduction
